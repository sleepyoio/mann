1960 Digital Equipment Corporation PDP-1

In 1960, the Digital Equipment Corporation (DEC) introduced the PDP-1 (Programmed Data Processor-1), an early minicomputer that would become a milestone in the history of computing. The PDP-1 was revolutionary for its time, representing a significant departure from the monolithic, room-filling mainframes that dominated the computing landscape

1964 IBM System 360

The system utilized Solid Logic Technology (SLT), an early form of integrated circuits. This innovation allowed IBM to produce more reliable and compact systems compared to earlier vacuum tube-based computers. The System/360 also supported scientific and commercial applications, making it versatile enough to cater to various industries.
1977 Commodore PET

The PET’s keyboard was designed with a calculator-style layout that is intuitive for users transitioning from traditional office equipment to computer systems. Including a user-friendly interface and comprehensive documentation made it an attractive option for educational institutions

TIMELINE OF COMPUTERS

1600s-1673

Analog computers gained popularity as devices for mathematical and statistical computations, using methods like logarithmic expressions, statistics, and decimals. John Napier invented the manually operated Napier's Bones calculator. The first automated calculator, the Pascaline, was created by French mathematician Blaise Pascal. This device was later improved by Gottfried Wilhelm Leibniz, who developed the Stepped Reckoner, also known as the Leibniz wheel.

1800s - 1890

Joseph Marie Jacquard invented a loom using wooden punch cards to automatically design woven textiles, a concept later adopted by early computers. Mathematician Charles Babbage created a steam-powered calculator and developed the difference engine to perform a wide range of computations, from simple math to complex astronomy. Herman Hollerith invented punch card technology for the 1880 U.S. census. Lady Ada Lovelace wrote the first computer program, consisting of user-generated instructions, taking three years to complete.

1900s-1950

Alan Turing proposed the idea of a universal machine. Hewlett-Packard was founded by Bill Hewlett and David Packard in a Palo Alto, California, garage, where they initially developed the HP 200A Audio Oscillator. George Stibitz suggested using a delay-based calculator for a project. In 1947, Bell Laboratories' Walter Brattain, William Shockley, and John Bardeen invented the transistor. The Standards Eastern Automatic Computer (SEAC), built in Washington, DC, became the United States' first operational stored-program computer.

1960s-1970

The American Standard Code for Information Exchange (ASCII) introduced the official computer character encoding standard. Douglas Engelbart demonstrated a prototype of the modern computer, featuring a mouse and graphical user interface (GUI). Alan Shugart and a team of IBM engineers created the floppy disk, allowing data sharing between computers. With the formal availability of personal computers, the Scelbi & Mark-8 Altair, IBM 5100, and Radio Shack's TRS-80 were among the first on the market. MicroPro International introduced WordStar, a word processing application.

2000-2010

In 2000, the USB flash drive was first released, providing a new method for data storage. Apple introduced the Mac OS X software platform, followed shortly by Microsoft's release of Windows XP. Apple then launched the MacBook Pro, their first mobile Intel-based dual-core computer. Microsoft later released Windows 7.
